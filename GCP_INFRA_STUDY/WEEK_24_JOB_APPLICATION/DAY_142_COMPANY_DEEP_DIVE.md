# Day 142 — Deep Dive 2 Companies

> **Week 24 · Job Application** | Prep 30 min | Activity 60 min | Revision 15 min | Quiz 15 min
>
> **Objective:** Research two target companies in depth — their tech stack, culture, interview process, and recent news — so you can deliver compelling, company-specific answers in interviews and cover letters.

---

## Part 1 — Concept: Why Company Research Wins Interviews (30 min)

### The Research Advantage

Candidates who demonstrate company knowledge get hired at significantly higher rates:

| Candidate Type | Typical Outcome |
|---|---|
| Generic answers, no company knowledge | Rejected: "Not a culture fit" or "Didn't seem interested" |
| Knows the product but not the tech | Passed phone screen, stuck in technical rounds |
| Knows the tech stack and recent initiatives | Strong interviews, asked better questions, stands out |
| Knows the team, tech, culture, and challenges | Gets offers: "They clearly did their homework" |

### The Company Research Template

For each target company, gather information in these categories:

| Category | What to Find | Where to Look |
|---|---|---|
| **Company basics** | Size, revenue, industry, HQ location, UK presence | Company website, LinkedIn, Glassdoor |
| **Tech stack** | Cloud provider, IaC tools, languages, platforms | Job postings, engineering blog, StackShare, GitHub |
| **Engineering culture** | Agile/waterfall, DevOps maturity, on-call practices | Engineering blog, conference talks, Glassdoor reviews |
| **Recent news** | Product launches, acquisitions, expansions, challenges | Google News, company blog, LinkedIn posts |
| **Interview process** | Rounds, format, timeline, typical questions | Glassdoor interviews, Blind, LinkedIn connections |
| **Team structure** | Team size, hiring manager, reporting lines | LinkedIn, job description, recruiter conversations |
| **Growth trajectory** | Hiring volume, new offices, revenue trends | LinkedIn jobs page, press releases, Companies House |
| **Values & mission** | Official values, how they practice them | About page, careers page, employee reviews |

### The "Why This Company?" Framework

Every interview will ask some version of "Why do you want to work here?" A strong answer has three components:

**1. Company-specific reason (not generic flattery)**
- Weak: "You're a great company with an innovative culture"
- Strong: "Your engineering blog post about migrating to multi-region GKE showed an approach to reliability that aligns with how I think about infrastructure"

**2. Role-specific connection**
- Weak: "I want to work in cloud"
- Strong: "This role's focus on Terraform-based infrastructure and GCP networking is exactly where my skills and interests are heading"

**3. Personal alignment**
- Weak: "It seems like a nice place to work"
- Strong: "I'm transitioning from traditional infrastructure to cloud, and your team's approach to upskilling engineers — like your internal cloud bootcamp — is the kind of environment where I'd thrive and contribute"

### Finding Inside Connections

| Method | How | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| **LinkedIn 1st connections** | Search your network for people at the company | Warm introduction, internal referral possible |
| **LinkedIn 2nd connections** | Ask mutual connections for an intro | "Hi [mutual], I see you're connected to [person] at [company]. Would you be comfortable making an intro?" |
| **Alumni networks** | Search your university or certification cohort | Shared background creates willingness to help |
| **Meetup / conference contacts** | Attend GCP/DevOps meetups in the UK | In-person connections are the strongest |
| **Direct LinkedIn message** | Message someone on the team you'd join | "Hi [Name], I'm applying for the [role] on your team and would love to learn more about what the team's working on." |
| **Recruiter relationship** | Build rapport with the recruiter assigned to your application | They're your advocate internally — help them help you |

### The Internal Referral Advantage

| Application Method | Typical Callback Rate |
|---|---|
| Cold application via job portal | 5-10% |
| Application with tailored resume + cover letter | 15-20% |
| Application with internal referral | 40-60% |
| Referral from someone on the hiring team | 60-80% |

Always try to get a referral before applying cold.

## Part 3 — Revision: Key Takeaways (15 min)

- **Company research separates average candidates from strong ones** — interviewers notice
- **Use the research template** — systematically cover tech stack, culture, news, interview process
- **"Why this company?" needs three components:** company-specific reason, role connection, personal alignment
- **Never give generic flattery** — "great culture" means nothing; cite specific examples
- **Internal referrals dramatically improve callback rates** — 40-60% vs 5-10% cold
- **LinkedIn is your primary research tool** — find connections, read profiles of team members, follow company pages
- **Engineering blogs reveal real tech culture** — what they write about = what they value
- **Glassdoor reviews need context** — filter by department and recency, look for patterns not individual complaints
- **Prepare 3 questions per company** — shows genuine interest and helps you evaluate fit
- **Outreach is a skill worth developing** — even if 80% of messages get no reply, the 20% that do can change your job search

## Part 4 — Quiz (15 min)

**Q1.** You're interviewing at a company that recently migrated from AWS to GCP. How do you use this in your interview?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Before the interview:**
1. Read any blog posts or press releases about the migration
2. Note which GCP services they mention
3. Understand common AWS-to-GCP migration challenges (IAM model differences, networking, Terraform provider changes)

**In the interview:**

1. **"Why this company?" answer:** "I was excited to see your recent migration to GCP. Having just earned my ACE certification and built several GCP infrastructure projects with Terraform, I'd love to contribute to a team that's actively building their GCP presence."

2. **Show relevant knowledge:** "I understand migrating from AWS to GCP involves significant IAM restructuring — moving from AWS's policy-based model to GCP's role-based model with org hierarchy. My LDAP/identity management background gives me a strong foundation for those kinds of access management transitions."

3. **Ask informed questions:**
   - "How far along is the migration? Are some workloads still on AWS?"
   - "Are you using Terraform for both clouds during the transition?"
   - "What's been the biggest challenge in the migration so far?"

4. **Connect your experience:** "My 6 years managing Linux infrastructure means I understand the complexity of production migrations — the planning, testing, and rollback strategies needed. I've applied that same discipline to my GCP projects."

This demonstrates: you did your homework, you understand the technical challenges, and you can connect your experience to their specific situation.
</details>

**Q2.** How do you research a company's tech stack when they don't have an engineering blog?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Alternative research methods:**

1. **Job postings (current and past):**
   - Search their careers page and LinkedIn jobs for all engineering roles
   - "Required: Terraform, GCP, Kubernetes, Python" — this IS their tech stack
   - Look at multiple roles: the overlap between them reveals core technologies

2. **LinkedIn employee profiles:**
   - Search "Site Reliability Engineer at [Company]" or "Cloud Engineer at [Company]"
   - Their skills and experience sections reveal what they actually use
   - Recent hires' backgrounds suggest what the team values

3. **StackShare / BuiltWith:**
   - Some companies list their stack on StackShare
   - BuiltWith can reveal frontend/web technologies

4. **GitHub organisation:**
   - If they have a public GitHub org, their repos show languages, tools, and practices
   - Check if they contribute to open-source projects

5. **Conference talks:**
   - Search YouTube for "[Company] + infrastructure" or "[Company] + GCP"
   - Engineers often present their architecture at conferences

6. **Glassdoor interview questions:**
   - Technical interview questions reveal what they test for
   - "They asked about Terraform state management" → they use Terraform

7. **Ask the recruiter:**
   - "Could you tell me more about the team's tech stack? I'd like to prepare relevant examples for the technical interview."
   - Recruiters expect this question and usually answer it

8. **Ask in informational interviews:**
   - If you have any connection at the company, ask about the tools they use daily
</details>

**Q3.** You have no connections at a company you want to apply to. What's your strategy?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Systematic approach to building connections from zero:**

1. **LinkedIn search — find the team:**
   - Search "[Company] Cloud Engineer" or "[Company] Infrastructure"
   - Identify 3-5 people who might be on the team you'd join
   - Check for 2nd-degree connections (mutual contacts)

2. **2nd-degree warm introductions:**
   - If you share a mutual connection, ask for an intro
   - "Hi [mutual], I noticed you're connected to [person] at [company]. I'm applying for a cloud role there. Would you be willing to make an introduction?"

3. **Cold outreach (it works more than you think):**
   - Message 3-5 people on the team — expect 1-2 replies
   - Keep it short, specific, and low-pressure
   - Don't ask for a referral in the first message — just ask for information

4. **Attend events they sponsor or speak at:**
   - Look for GCP meetups, DevOps London events, or conferences
   - Engage with speakers afterwards — this is legitimate networking

5. **Engage with their content:**
   - Like/comment on their team's LinkedIn posts thoughtfully
   - If they write technical articles, share with your own commentary
   - After 2-3 genuine interactions, a direct message feels natural

6. **Apply with a targeted cover letter:**
   - If no connection is possible, a research-driven cover letter is your next best option
   - Reference specific things about their tech stack, culture, or recent work

**Timeline:** Start connection-building 1-2 weeks before applying. Don't rush it.
</details>

**Q4.** What are the best questions to ask at the end of an interview, and what should you NEVER ask?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Strong questions (show you've done research and think ahead):**

1. "What does the team's infrastructure look like currently, and what are the biggest priorities for the next 6-12 months?" — Shows you're thinking about contributing, not just getting a job

2. "How does the team approach incident response and on-call? What does a typical on-call rotation look like?" — Shows you understand operational responsibility

3. "I noticed your team recently [migrated to GCP / adopted Terraform / built X]. What lessons have you learned from that?" — Shows you researched the company

4. "How do you support engineers who are transitioning from traditional infrastructure to cloud?" — Directly relevant to your situation, and shows self-awareness

5. "What would success look like for this role in the first 90 days?" — Shows you're already thinking about delivering value

**Never ask (signals wrong priorities):**

- "What's the salary?" — Save for recruiter/HR, not technical interviews
- "How many hours do people actually work?" — Signals you're watching the clock
- "When can I work from home?" — Fine to ask HR, but not your first question to the hiring manager
- "I don't have any questions" — Always signals disinterest; always have at least 2 questions
- "Did I get the job?" — Too early; shows impatience
- "What does your company actually do?" — Shows you didn't research at all

**Pro tip:** Prepare 5 questions but expect to ask only 2-3 (some will be answered during the interview). Adjust based on what you've learned during the conversation.
</details>
