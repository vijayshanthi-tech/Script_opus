### Cover Letter Template

Most applications ask for a cover letter. A good one is **personalised but follows a formula:**

**Structure: 4 Paragraphs, 250-300 Words**

```
[Your Name]
[Your Email] | [Your LinkedIn URL] | [Your GitHub URL]
[Date]

[Hiring Manager Name / "Hiring Team"]
[Company Name]
[Role Title]

Dear [Hiring Manager / Hiring Team],

PARAGRAPH 1 — HOOK (2-3 sentences)
I'm writing to express my interest in the [Role Title] position at [Company]. 
With [X years] of Linux infrastructure experience and a Google Cloud ACE 
certification, I bring a blend of deep systems expertise and hands-on cloud 
engineering skills that directly align with your team's needs.

PARAGRAPH 2 — EVIDENCE (3-4 sentences)
In my current role, I [strongest relevant achievement with metric]. I've 
complemented my operational experience by building a portfolio of GCP projects 
including [Project 1 — brief], [Project 2 — brief], and [Project 3 — brief], 
all implemented in Terraform and documented on GitHub. My 3 years of RHDS/LDAP 
directory services experience translates directly to the IAM and access 
management aspects of this role.

PARAGRAPH 3 — WHY THIS COMPANY (2-3 sentences)
I'm particularly drawn to [Company] because [specific reason — their tech stack, 
mission, recent project, blog post, culture]. [How your skills/values align with 
their specific needs or direction].

PARAGRAPH 4 — CLOSE (2 sentences)
I'd welcome the opportunity to discuss how my infrastructure background and 
cloud engineering skills can contribute to [Company]'s team. I'm available at 
[email] and look forward to connecting.

Best regards,
[Your Name]
```

### Cover Letter Personalisation Points

| What to Personalise | Where to Find It | Time |
|---|---|---|
| **Company name + role** | Job posting | 1 min |
| **Hiring manager name** | LinkedIn, company site | 3 min |
| **"Why this company" paragraph** | Company blog, news, tech blog | 5 min |
| **Achievement highlight** | Match to JD's top requirement | 3 min |
| **Project reference** | Your most JD-relevant project | 2 min |

Total personalisation time: ~15 minutes per cover letter.

### The 30-Second Elevator Pitch

For networking, career fairs, or the "tell me about yourself" interview opener.

**Formula:**

```
"I'm a [role title] with [X years] of [key experience]. 
I recently [key credential or achievement]. 
Right now, I'm [what you're doing/looking for]. 
What I'm most excited about is [value you bring or passion area]."
```

**Example:**

> "I'm a Cloud Infrastructure Engineer with 6 years of Linux systems administration experience. I recently earned my Google Cloud ACE certification and built a portfolio of GCP infrastructure projects using Terraform — covering networking, monitoring, and IAM. I'm currently looking for cloud infrastructure or SRE roles where I can combine my deep Linux expertise with cloud-native engineering. What excites me most is designing reliable, automated infrastructure that teams can trust."

**Practice this until it sounds natural, not scripted.** Record yourself and listen back.

## Part 4 — Quiz (15 min)

**Q1.** Why should you write a cover letter even when the application says "optional"?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

Three reasons: (1) **Differentiation** — the majority of candidates skip the optional cover letter. Including one immediately puts you in a smaller, more committed pool. (2) **Narrative space** — a cover letter lets you tell the story that a resume can't: why you're transitioning, why this specific company, how your background connects to the role. This is especially important for career transitioners where the resume alone might raise questions. (3) **"Why this company" signal** — a personalised cover letter that references the company's specific work, tech stack, or values shows you've done research and are genuinely interested, not just mass-applying.

The exception: if an application explicitly says "do not include a cover letter," respect that.
</details>

**Q2.** Your elevator pitch is 60 seconds long. What should you cut to get it to 30 seconds?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Keep:** (1) Role title — "Cloud Infrastructure Engineer", (2) Key differentiator — "6 years Linux + ACE certified", (3) Current activity — "built GCP portfolio with Terraform", (4) One value statement — "I bring deep Linux expertise to cloud architecture"

**Cut:** (1) Listing technologies (save for questions), (2) Detailed project descriptions (too much info), (3) Career history chronology ("In 2018 I started as..."), (4) Multiple achievements (pick your strongest one), (5) Filler phrases ("I'm really passionate about...", "I believe that...")

**Test:** If someone asked "what do you do?" at a networking event, what are the **four essential facts** they need? Role, experience level, credential, and what makes you different. Everything else is follow-up conversation.
</details>

**Q3.** What should happen 2 weeks after you submit a job application?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Follow up.** Send a short, professional follow-up message to:
1. **The recruiter** (if you know who they are — check LinkedIn)
2. **The hiring manager** (if you can find them)
3. **Through the ATS** (if there's a "message" or "update" option)

**Follow-up template:**

> "Hi [Name], I applied for the [Role] position at [Company] on [date] and wanted to follow up to reiterate my interest. With my ACE certification and hands-on GCP infrastructure portfolio, I believe I'd be a strong fit for the team. I'm happy to provide any additional information. Thank you for your time."

**Rules:** (1) Keep it to 3-4 sentences, (2) Don't apologise for following up, (3) Reference one specific skill or achievement, (4) Only follow up once — never badger, (5) Log the follow-up in your tracker.
</details>

**Q4.** You've assembled your complete kit. Before you start applying (Week 24), what final self-check should you perform?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**The "fresh eyes" review:**

1. **Close everything** and take a 24-hour break from your materials
2. **Re-read your resume** as if you're the hiring manager — does it answer "why should I interview this person?"
3. **Visit your GitHub profile** logged out — does it look professional? Do all links work?
4. **Read your LinkedIn About section** on mobile — does it render well? Is the first sentence compelling?
5. **Say your elevator pitch to a friend or family member** — can they tell what role you're looking for?
6. **Send your resume to 1-2 trusted people** for honest feedback
7. **Check all links:** GitHub repos, LinkedIn URL, email address
8. **Ensure consistency:** job titles, dates, and claims match across resume, LinkedIn, and GitHub

Fix any issues found, then you're ready for Week 23 (Interview Prep) and Week 24 (Applications).
</details>
